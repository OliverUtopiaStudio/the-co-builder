# Content Build — Video Script & Outline

**Purpose:** Teach fellows how to go from zero to a live, funnel-minded site: setup (Claude, git, design, Vercel, domain), iteration based on what the customer needs to learn, and funnel fundamentals so the site drives Demos and sign ups.

**Use:** Record as Loom (or similar); link via `asset_media` for Module 101 / Content Build. Approx. 12–18 min.

---

## Part 1 — Setup path (example: Claude → Git → Design → Vercel → Domain)

**Goal:** One clear, repeatable path so anyone can get a site live and owned.

### 1.1 Claude space

- **What:** A dedicated project in Claude (or Cursor) where you keep prompts, specs, and copy for the site.
- **Why:** Keeps “what we’re building” and “what we’re saying” in one place; AI can stay consistent with your positioning.
- **How:** Create a project; add a short brief (problem, audience, one key action). Use it for generating copy, section outlines, and later iteration notes.

### 1.2 Git

- **What:** Version control for the codebase (and often the design/content repo).
- **Why:** Track changes, roll back, collaborate; required for Vercel and professional workflows.
- **How:** Init a repo, commit often (e.g. “hero section”, “pricing copy”), push to GitHub/GitLab. Branch only if you’re doing a big experiment.

### 1.3 Design the site

- **What:** Structure and layout before or alongside code: hero, problem/solution, proof, single primary CTA.
- **Why:** Stops “building pages” and focuses on one story and one action.
- **How:** Lo-fi is enough: Figma, Notion, or even a doc. Define: one headline, one subhead, 2–3 proof points, one button (e.g. “Book a demo” or “Join waitlist”). No extra pages until this works.

### 1.4 Push to Vercel

- **What:** Deploy the site from your Git repo so it’s live on a URL.
- **Why:** Fast, free tier, automatic deploys on push; no server management.
- **How:** Connect GitHub to Vercel, point at the repo, deploy. Use preview URLs for iterations before merging to main.

### 1.5 Buy a domain

- **What:** A real domain (e.g. yourventure.com) that you own and point at Vercel.
- **Why:** Credibility and a stable URL for demos, emails, and ads.
- **How:** Register the domain (Registrar e.g. Namecheap, Cloudflare, Vercel); add it in Vercel and follow DNS instructions. SSL is automatic.

**Takeaway:** Claude space (brief + copy) → Git (code + history) → simple design (one story, one CTA) → Vercel (live) → domain (owned). That’s the path.

## Part 2 — Iterate based on what the customer wants to understand

**Goal:** The site should teach and persuade. Iteration is driven by “what does the visitor need to understand before they take the action we want?”

### 2.1 What “content” is for at this stage

- Content isn’t decoration. It answers: *What is this? Who is it for? Why should I care? What do I do next?*
- If the visitor doesn’t understand, they don’t convert. So “what the customer wants to understand” = what we need to teach in copy and structure.

### 2.2 Learning from real people

- **Interviews and demos:** After calls, note where people asked “What do you mean by…?” or “How does that work?” Those are content gaps.
- **Objections:** “Is this for people like me?” “How is this different from X?” Turn those into clear sections or lines on the site.
- **Questions before the CTA:** If they need to understand pricing, use case, or security before clicking “Book a demo,” add that above the fold or one scroll down.

### 2.3 What to change when you iterate

- **Headline/subhead:** Do they get “what it is” and “for whom” in 5 seconds? If not, tighten or reframe.
- **Proof:** Do they need a case study, a quote, or a “how it works” before they trust the CTA? Add the smallest proof that moves the needle.
- **One CTA:** Keep a single primary action. Change the label if needed (e.g. “See a demo” vs “Get early access”) based on what converts in demos or waitlist.

### 2.4 Teaching without over-explaining

- Lead with the outcome and the “for whom”; details can live one click away (e.g. “How it works” or a short doc).
- Use the funnel (Part 3) to decide what belongs on the homepage vs deeper pages.

**Takeaway:** Iterate the site by asking “What did the customer need to understand before they were ready to act?” and then editing copy and structure to teach that.

## Part 3 — Funnel fundamentals: Demos and sign ups

**Goal:** At this stage, the site’s job is to support Demos and sign ups — not to do everything.

### 3.1 What a funnel is at this stage

- **Awareness:** They landed on the site (organic, referral, or outbound).
- **Interest:** They understand what you do and for whom.
- **Consideration:** They see proof and relevance (social proof, use case, or “how it works”).
- **Action:** They book a demo or sign up (waitlist / early access).

- The site is the top and middle of the funnel: it gets them to that one action. Closing happens in the call or email, not on the page.

### 3.2 One primary action

- **Demos:** Primary CTA = “Book a demo” or “See a demo.” The page should answer “Why book?” and “What will I see?”
- **Sign ups:** Primary CTA = “Join waitlist” or “Get early access.” The page should answer “Why join?” and “What happens next?”
- Don’t mix two primary CTAs above the fold. One action per stage.

### 3.3 What to measure (basics)

- **Traffic:** How many hit the site (by source if possible).
- **Scroll / time:** Do they read the key section before the CTA?
- **Clicks on primary CTA:** How many click “Book a demo” or “Join waitlist?”
- **Conversions:** How many actually complete the form or booking?

- At this stage, focus on: *visits → CTA clicks → completed actions.* Fix the biggest drop (e.g. no CTA clicks → improve clarity; CTA clicks but no completion → fix form or calendar).

### 3.4 Copy and layout that serve the funnel

- **Hero:** Clear value prop + primary CTA. No jargon.
- **Just below:** Short “who it’s for” or “how it works” so they can self-qualify.
- **Proof:** One strong proof point (logo, quote, or result) before or next to the CTA.
- **Secondary CTA (optional):** e.g. “Learn more” to a page that then leads back to the primary CTA.

**Takeaway:** The site serves a function: get the right visitors to one action (Demos or sign ups). Design and iterate so that path is obvious and the content teaches what they need before they click.

---

## Summary for the fellow

1. **Setup:** Claude space (brief + copy) → Git (code) → simple design (one story, one CTA) → Vercel (deploy) → domain (own it).
2. **Iteration:** Use customer conversations to find what they needed to understand; update copy and structure so the site teaches that before the CTA.
3. **Funnel:** One primary action (Demos or sign ups); measure visits → CTA clicks → completions; keep hero, proof, and CTA aligned so the site clearly serves Demos/sign ups at this stage.
